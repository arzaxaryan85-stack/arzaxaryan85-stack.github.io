🏔️ Major Mountains:

Mount Ararat (5,137 m / 16,854 ft) — Symbolically significant in Armenian culture and tradition.
History of Ararat Mount Ararat is one of the most symbolic and sacred mountains in the history of mankind and especially in Armenian culture. It is located on the territory of modern Turkey, near the border with Armenia, and is the highest peak of the Armenian highlands.
📍 Geography
Geography Height of Greater Ararat: 5,137 m (16,854 ft) Little Ararat: 3,896 m Located in eastern Turkey, Igdir Province Located only ~30 km from the capital of Armenia, Yerevan. Visible from most points in the city.
📜 Historical and cultural significance
1. Biblical tradition - According to the Bible (Genesis 8:4), Noah's Ark "came to rest on the mountains of Ararat" after the Great Flood. In Jewish, Christian and Islamic tradition, Mount Ararat is associated with this episode. Although the Bible does not speak of a specific mountain, but of "the mountains of Ararat", later the tradition was fixed on this peak.
2. Armenian Mythology and Identity - Ararat is a national symbol of Armenia, despite the fact that it is located outside the modern territory of the country. It is depicted on the coat of arms of Armenia. In Armenian tradition, it is considered a sacred mountain, and many legends are associated with it. ❝ An Armenian may not live at the foot of Ararat, but Ararat lives in the heart of every Armenian. ❞
3. History of territorial changes - Before the Russo-Turkish War of 1828-1829 and especially until the 1920s, Ararat was part of the history of Armenia. After the Armenian Genocide of 1915 and the implementation of the Treaty of Kars (1921), the mountain ended up in Turkish territory. This fact remains painful for many Armenians, and the mountain often symbolizes the lost homeland.
4. Archaeology and expeditions - Many expeditions have tried to find the remains of the New Ark on the slopes of Ararat (especially since the 20th century). There have been reports of wooden structures at altitude, but none of the finds have been scientifically confirmed.
5. Military and strategic importance - The area around Mount Ararat is strategically important. The border between Turkey, Armenia and Iran runs nearby. Turkish military bases are located near the summit, and access to the mountain is limited.

Mount Aragats — The highest point in present-day Armenia
Mount Aragats is the highest mountain in modern-day Armenia, and one of the most iconic natural and cultural landmarks in the region. Mount Aragats is the highest mountain in modern-day Armenia, and one of the most iconic natural and cultural landmarks in the region.
📍 Basic Facts
Feature - Description Elevation - 4,090 meters (13,419 feet) | Location - Aragatsotn Province, northwest of Yerevan | Volcanic Type - Extinct stratovolcano | Number of Peaks - 4 (forming a massive caldera) | Highest Point - Northern peak (4,090 m)
🗻 Structure and Peaks
Mount Aragats is not a single-peaked mountain — it is a large volcanic massif with four summits arranged around a central crater: North Peak: Highest point (4,090 m) East Peak: 3,908 m South Peak: 3,879 m West Peak: 3,995 m The central crater is around 400 meters deep and partially filled with snow year-round.
🕰️ Historical & Cultural Significance
1. Name Origin
"Aragats" is believed to derive from "Ara", a legendary Armenian hero-king (as in Ara the Beautiful) and “gah”, meaning throne — “Throne of Ara.”
2. Spiritual and Mythological Importance
Considered a sacred mountain in Armenian culture. In local legends, Aragats is connected to Mount Ararat; folklore describes them as “brother and sister” mountains.
3. Historical Monuments Nearby
Mount Aragats is surrounded by significant medieval and ancient Armenian sites: 🏰 Amberd Fortress (7th century): A castle built on the slopes of Aragats 🕍 Vahramashen Church (11th century): Next to Amberd 🛰️ Byurakan Observatory: Major scientific center located on the southern slope ⛪ Mastara Church: One of Armenia’s oldest standing churches nearby
🌄 Modern Significance
🔭 Science & Research
Byurakan Astrophysical Observatory (founded by Viktor Ambartsumian) is a key center for space research in the former USSR and still operates today.
🥾 Tourism & Hiking
Popular among climbers and hikers, especially in summer Hike to the crater rim and summit can be challenging due to snow, steep trails, and elevation Lake Kari (3,200 m) is a beautiful high-altitude lake near the summit, often the starting point for hikes
❄️ Climate and Conditions
Snow-covered most of the year; summit often accessible June–September Sudden weather changes are common Glacier remnants exist on the northern slopes
📸 Symbolic Role in Armenia
While Mount Ararat remains the national symbol (despite lying in Turkey), Mount Aragats is the tallest mountain on Armenian soil today. Seen as a symbol of endurance, continuity, and heritage.

🌊 1. Lake Sevan
📍 Location - Republic of Armenia (Gegharkunik region)
Type - Freshwater, highland
📏 Area - ~1,242 km²
🏔 Altitude - ~1,900 m
🐟 Ecosystem - Sevan trout (ishkhan), crayfish, waterfowl
🏛 History and culture - Sevanavank (IX century), ancient settlements
📌 Interesting facts:
One of the largest freshwater high-mountain reservoirs in Eurasia. During the Soviet era, the needs for energy supply and irrigation were greatly reduced (the water level dropped by 20 m). In the 21st century, a project is underway to restore the water level and ecosystem.

🌊2. Lake Van
📍 Location - Eastern Turkey (historical Vaspurakan Armenia)
🌊 Type - Salty, alkaline, endorheic
📏 Area - ~3.755 km²
🏔 Altitude - ~1.640 m
🐟 Ecosystem - Endemic fish (Van balığı), flamingo
🏛 History and culture - Akhtamar Island, 10th century Armenian monastery
📌 Interesting facts:
The largest lake in Turkey and the Armenian Highlands. The water is saturated with soda and salts - you can't drown in it. In ancient times - the center of the Armenian principality and kingdom of Urartu, later - the Armenian kingdom of Vaspurakan. Akhtamar Monastery is a unique example of Armenian architecture of the 10th century.

🌊 3.Lake Urmia
📍 Location - North-western Iran (Southern Azerbaijan, historical Peroarmenia)
🌊 Type - Salty, non-flowing
📏 Area - ~5,200 km² (in the past), ~1,000 km² or less nowadays
🏔 Altitude above sea level - ~1.275 m
🐟 Ecosystem - Solonchak, artemia (salt crabs), flamingo
🏛 History and culture - In ancient times - part of the Armenian and Persian kingdoms
📌 Interesting facts:
The second saltiest lake after the Dead Sea. Since the 1990s, it has almost disappeared due to irrigation and climate change. Iran is conducting projects to save it, but restoration is difficult. The name “Urmia” may be related to Urartian or Armenian origins.

🌐 Cultural significance
All three lakes were historically part of the Armenian civilization: Near Sevan - monasteries and fortresses. Van - the capital of the Armenian kingdom of Vaspurakan, Armenian population before the genocide of 1915. Urmia - eastern Armenian dioceses, large communities until the beginning of the 20th century.

The Armenian Highland

is a high region at the junction of Asia Minor, the Caucasus, and Iran, covering the territories of modern Armenia, Eastern Turkey, Nakhichevan, northwestern Iran, and southern Georgia. It is one of the oldest inhabited areas of Eurasia, formed as a result of intense volcanic activity and tectonic processes. The average altitude of the plateau is about 1,500–2,500 meters above sea level, with many extinct volcanoes, basalt plateaus, and high-mountain lakes. Its key peaks are Ararat, Aragats, Sipan, and Nemrut, and its largest lakes are Van, Sevan, and Urmia.
The Armenian Highlands historically played the role of a link between Mesopotamia, Iran and Transcaucasia. It was here that some of the first agricultural cultures of the Neolithic period arose. As early as the 7th–6th millennia BC, settlements with developed forms of agriculture, cattle breeding and crafts appeared in this region. In the following millennia, the plateau became part of the most ancient cultural areas: Chalcolithic, Early Bronze and Late Bronze civilizations. The region was distinguished by a diversity of tribes, local states and rich resource potential, including metallurgical centers.
In the 9th–7th centuries BC, the kingdom of Urartu existed in the eastern part of the plateau, strengthening the system of irrigation, urban construction and state administration. This state interacted with Assyria and Media and left a rich archaeological and epigraphic heritage. After the collapse of Urartu, the territory of the Armenian Highlands became an arena of rivalry between various powers and local ethnic groups.
Throughout antiquity and classical times, the region was a crossroads of trade, military campaigns and cultural exchanges. Caravan routes passed through the Armenian Highlands, connecting Western Asia with the Black Sea and Caspian regions. At different times it was within the sphere of influence of Achaemenid Persia, the Hellenistic kingdoms, Rome, Byzantium, the Arab Caliphate, the Seljuk and Ottoman Empires, and the Russian Empire.
Despite the change of ruling empires, the Armenian Highland retained its cultural and geopolitical integrity thanks to its landscape, which created stable regional structures and forms of life. It was its mountainous isolation and natural features that contributed to the development of local cultures, crafts, writing and religious traditions.
By the beginning of the 20th century, a significant part of the Armenian Highlands was part of the Ottoman and Persian empires, and later Turkey, Iran and the USSR. As a result of wars, genocide, migrations and resettlements, the ethnic and cultural appearance of the region changed significantly, but its historical significance as one of the centers of the ancient world and natural and cultural landscape remains to this day.
Today, the Armenian Highland is a unique geographical and historical-cultural region with a rich archaeological heritage, landscape diversity and a multi-thousand-year history that is important not only for the peoples of the Caucasus and the Middle East, but also for the entire world civilization.

The Orontids were initially satraps (governors) under the Median and Achaemenid empires, but over time they gained semi-independence and eventually full kingship, especially after the fall of the Achaemenid Empire in 330 BC.

🧿 Origins and Role
The Orontids were of either Iranian (Median) or Armenian nobility origin, closely connected to the Achaemenid royal family. Their rule marked the beginning of Armenian statehood. They founded major political centers like Erebuni (modern Yerevan), Armavir, and later Yerazgavors. They ruled over Armenia, Sophene (Tzopk), and Commagene, depending on the period.

⚖️ Legacy of the Orontids
First to unify parts of historic Armenia under a native dynasty. Maintained Armenian identity under Persian and later Macedonian rule. Their era saw the foundation of key cities and early forms of Armenian aristocracy. The later Kings of Commagene (a Greco-Iranian kingdom) also claimed descent from the Orontids.

🛑End of the Orontid Dynasty – c. 200 BC
Around 201–200 BC, the Orontid line was overthrown by Artashes I, the founder of the Artashesid (Artaxiad) dynasty. Artashes established a stronger, centralized Armenian kingdom with Hellenistic influence and minted Armenian coins.

🦁 Artashesids - 189–12. Don e.

1. Artashes I (Artaxias I) – r. 189–160 BC Founder of the Artaxiad dynasty. United the Armenian lands into one kingdom after the fall of the Seleucid influence. Founded the capital Artashat (with the help of Hannibal, according to legend).
2. Artavazd I – r. 160–115 BC Son of Artashes I. Continued centralization of power and development of Armenian statehood.
3. Tigranes I – r. c. 115–95 BC Possibly ruled jointly or as a client of Parthia; transitional king.
4. Tigranes II "The Great" (Tigran Mets) – r. 95–55 BC The most powerful Armenian king in history. Expanded Armenia into an empire that included parts of Syria, Mesopotamia, and the Caucasus. Capital at Tigranocerta. Fought wars with Rome (e.g. against Lucullus and Pompey).
5. Artavazd II – r. 55–34 BC Son of Tigranes the Great. Allied with both Rome and Parthia at different times. Captured and executed by Mark Antony after the Parthian campaign.
6. Artashes II – r. 34–20 BC Installed by the Parthians. Later deposed by the Romans.
7. Tigranes III – r. 20–8 BC Installed by Augustus Caesar. Ruled under Roman influence; relative peace during his reign.
8. Tigranes IV – r. 8–5 BC and 2 BC–1 AD Co-ruled with his sister-wife Erato. Reign marked by turmoil and shifting allegiances between Rome and Parthia.
9. Erato – r. Co-regent with Tigranes IV and briefly on her own One of the few female rulers in Armenian history. Attempted to maintain royal legitimacy through Artaxiad lineage.
10. Tigranes V – r. 6–12 AD Final Artaxiad king. Appointed by the Romans. After his reign, Armenia became more directly influenced by Rome and Parthia until the Arshakuni Dynasty took over in 66 AD.

🛑 End of the Artashesid Dynasty (12 AD) The dynasty ended after Tigranes V, and Armenia entered a period of instability with frequent interference by Rome and Parthia. The Arshakuni Dynasty later emerged as the new ruling house, beginning with Tiridates I in 66 AD.

👑 Bagratuni / Bagratid Dynasty Overview

Name: Bagratuni (Բագրատունի); also known as Bagratid (Western scholarly term), Bagratian Rule: As Princes (Ishkhans): c. 7th century – 885 AD As Kings of Armenia: 885–1045 AD Capital cities: Bagaran, Shirakavan, Kars, and especially Ani Language: Classical Armenian (Grabar), with Greek and Arabic influence
🧬 Origins of the Bagratuni Dynasty
Claimed descent from Biblical King David, similar to other noble dynasties like the Georgian Bagrationi. Rose to prominence under Arab Caliphate rule, serving as sparapets (commanders-in-chief) and nakharars (princes). Became presiding princes (ishkhans) under Byzantine and Arab dominion before asserting full independence in 885.

⚔️ Decline and Fall
After Gagik II was forced into exile in 1045, Armenia came under Byzantine control. Soon after, the Seljuk Turks invaded (Battle of Manzikert in 1071), further ending Armenian autonomy in the region. Bagratuni branches continued ruling in Kars, Tao, and Syunik, and some joined Georgian nobility.

🏛️ Cultural and Historical Legacy
Golden Age of Armenian Architecture: Domed churches, cross-in-square plans, khachkars. Political Renaissance: Reunification of Armenian regions after centuries of foreign rule. Art, literature, and theology: Flourished under church patronage. Bagratuni family continues through Georgian Bagrationi royal line.

🏰 Rubinian (Roupenid) Dynasty – Princes of Cilician Armenia


Reign as Princes: 1080 – 1198 AD Capital(s): Vahka (later Sis, Anazarbus) Language: Armenian (Grabar), with Greek, Latin, Arabic influences Religion: Armenian Apostolic Church (with increasing Catholic influence in later years)

📜 List of Rubinian Princes of Armenian Cilicia
1 Ruben I c. 1080–1095 - Founder of the dynasty. Former noble of Bagratuni descent. Rebelled against Byzantine rule and established Armenian control in the Taurus Mountains.
2 Constantine I (Kostandin I) 1095–1100 - Son of Ruben I; expanded territory and secured Armenian authority in Cilicia.
3 Thoros I 1100–1129 - Consolidated power; repelled Seljuk and Byzantine attacks; fortified Vahka and other strongholds.
4 Leon I (Leo I) 1129–1137 - Continued expansion; imprisoned by the Byzantines in 1137; died in captivity.
5 Thoros II 1145–1169 - Son of Leon I; returned from exile and restored Armenian independence. One of the most effective military leaders of the dynasty.
6 Mleh 1169–1175 - Brother of Thoros II; controversial ruler, converted to Islam temporarily, allied with Nur ad-Din of Aleppo. Deposed and assassinated.
7 Roupen II 1175–1187 - Son of Thoros II; ruled as a minor under regency; died young.
8 Leo II (Prince) 1187–1198 - Nephew of Thoros II; expanded and strengthened the principality, paving the way for kingship.
– Leo I the Magnificent (as King) 1198–1219 - Crowned King of Armenia in 1198 by the Holy Roman Empire and the Papacy — marking the end of the principality and the rise of the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia.

🕊️ Historical Background
After the fall of the Bagratuni Kingdom in 1045 and the Seljuk invasion (Battle of Manzikert, 1071), many Armenian nobles and people migrated to Cilicia. Ruben I, a noble claiming descent from the Bagratids, established a mountain stronghold and gradually expanded Armenian authority. The Rubinians built alliances with Crusader states, Byzantium, and later Western Europe. By the late 12th century, under Leo II, Cilicia was recognized as a regional Christian power.

🔚 End of the Rubinian Dynasty (as ruling line)
In 1198, Leo II was crowned King Leo I, marking the transition from prince to king and the beginning of the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia. The Rubinians continued as kings until the Hethumid Dynasty took power in 1252 through dynastic marriage.

🏛️ Legacy of the Rubinians
Founders of Cilician Armenian independence, diplomacy, and kingship. Established alliances with Crusaders, the Papacy, and Europe. Defended Christian Armenia in a hostile Islamic and Byzantine frontier. Helped preserve Armenian culture, literature, and autonomy during the Middle Ages.

🛑 1375 – Fall of the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia

King Levon VI (Leo V) Lusignan was the last reigning king. After his capture by the Mamluks, the Armenian monarchy ceased to exist as a sovereign state. However, titular claims to the Armenian crown were carried on by the Lusignan kings of Cyprus, then by the House of Savoy and others.

👑 Titular “Kings of Armenia” (1375–20th century)

⚜️ Lusignan Dynasty (Cyprus branch)
After 1375, the Kings of Cyprus (Lusignans) continued to style themselves as “King of Cyprus, Jerusalem, and Armenia.” James I of Cyprus d. 1398 Nephew of Leo V; claimed title after Leo’s death. Successive Lusignan kings of Cyprus 14th–15th centuries Continued using “King of Armenia” in official titles.
🇮🇹 House of Savoy
In 1485, after Lusignan extinction, the House of Savoy inherited the titular claim through Charlotte of Cyprus, last queen of the Lusignan line. Savoy rulers (later Kings of Italy) used the title until the 20th century: Victor Emmanuel II (first king of unified Italy) Victor Emmanuel III Umberto II (last King of Italy, until 1946)
⚠️ These titles were ceremonial and symbolic, with no control over Armenian lands.

While Armenia is a republic and has no monarchy, symbolic or genealogical claims continue to be discussed by historians and enthusiasts.

Some historical claimants include: House of Savoy (Italy) – formerly claimed the title through Lusignan descent. Lusignan descendants (France, Cyprus) – minor noble branches; no recognized claim. Georgian Bagrationi – distantly related to Armenian Bagratids; no formal claim.

religion

The religion of Armenia has an ancient and rich history, which goes back to pre-Christian times and played a huge role in the formation of Armenian identity.



📜 Pre-Christian religion (until IV century AD)

Until the adoption of Christianity, Armenians professed paganism, including: Armenian pantheon of gods based on Urartian, Persian and Greek influences. The main god is Aramazd (an analogue of Zeus), the god of wisdom and creation. Anahit is the goddess of fertility and motherhood. Вахагн — бог грома и войны (Armenian "Hercules"). There were temples, priests, sacrifices.



Zoroastrianism.




✝️ Acceptance of Christianity (301 AD)

Armenia became the first state in the world to officially accept Christianity as a state religion. Initiator — St. Gregory the Enlightener and Tsar Trdat III. The Armenian Apostolic Church was born, which still plays an important role.



St. Grigor Lusavorich church




Middle Ages

The Armenian Church remained independent from Byzantium and Rome, rejecting the Chalcedon Cathedral (451). Armenia and the church were closely related: the church preserved culture, language and traditions under foreign rule (Arabs, Seljuks, Mongols, Turks). Outstanding monasteries were built: Sevanavank, Tatev, Gegard, Khor Virap.


Ottoman and Persian periods (XVI-XIX centuries)

Armenians were oppressed, but the church continued to function. Echmiadzin, the main monastery and residence of the Catholicos of all Armenians, became the center of spiritual life.


Armenian diaspora and genocide

After the Armenian genocide in 1915 under the Ottoman Empire, the church became a support for Armenians scattered around the world. Many spiritual centers were created in Lebanon, USA, France and others.


Soviet period (1920-1991)

Religion was limited by the Soviet atheistic regime. However, the Armenian Church partially survived, especially during the Second World War and in the post-war years.


Modern Armenia (since 1991)

After gaining independence, religion again became an important part of life. Armenian Apostolic Church — dominant. There are also communities of Catholics, Protestants, Jews, and Muslims (in a very limited number). The Constitution guarantees freedom of religion, but the church has a special status.


Basic fact

The main church: Echmiadzin cathedral — one of the oldest in the world. Main spiritual leader: Catholicos of all Armenians (now Garegin II). The majority of Armenians (more than 90%) identify themselves as Christians of the Apostolic Church.


genocide

The Armenian Genocide was the systematic extermination of the Armenian population of the Ottoman Empire from 1915 to 1923. This tragic event is recognized by many countries and historians as genocide, that is, the deliberate extermination of an entire people.

What happened?
Since 1915 (during World War I), the Ottoman government, especially the Young Turks, began mass deportations and killings of Armenians. At first, Armenian leaders, intellectuals, and men of military age were arrested and killed. Then hundreds of thousands of women, children and old people were forcibly sent to the Syrian desert - in conditions where it was almost impossible to survive (death marches). According to various estimates, between 800 thousand and 1.5 million Armenians died.
The reasons were complex and multi-level:
Nationalism and the Fear of Empire Disintegration The Ottoman Empire was multinational, but by the early 20th century Turkish nationalism was on the rise. Armenians, as a Christian minority, were suspected of disloyalty.
Wartime and the Fear of the "Internal Enemy" During World War I, the Ottoman Empire fought against Russia. Some Armenians actually fought on the side of the Russian Empire. This became a pretext for declaring all Armenians potential "traitors."
The idea of "Turkization" The Young Turks wanted to create a single Turkic state - without Christians and "foreigners". Armenians, Greeks and Assyrians fell into this category.
Consequences
The Armenians were almost completely exterminated in historical Western Armenia (present-day Eastern Turkey). Those who survived scattered around the world, creating the Armenian Diaspora. Turkey still does not officially recognize the genocide, which remains a source of international conflict and pain for Armenians around the world.

literature

Armenian literature is one of the oldest and richest in the world. It covers almost 1700 years of written history, starting from the 5th century.

Ancient and medieval literature (V-XV centuries)

Golden age of Armenian literature (V century) Mesrop Mashtots - the inventor of the Armenian alphabet (~405) Movses Khorenatsi - "History of Armenia" (5 century) - fundamental work on history. Agafangel , Eznik Kohbatsi,Faustos Buzand - early historians and theologians. Main topic: history, religion, philosophy.
Epic poetry. " David of Sasun" (David of Sasun) - heroic epic (9th-10th centuries), transmitted orally. David is a national hero who fights for justice.

Middle Ages (XI-XVII centuries)

Armenian monasteries (for example, Sanakhin and Tatev) became centers of writing. Topics: theology, translations from Greek and Syriac, historical chronicles. Narekatsi (Grigor Narekatsi) is a great mystic and poet (10th century), author of "Books of Sorrowful Songs" - spiritual poetry about human suffering and the search for God.

New time (XVIII-XIX century)

Development of printing, especially in Venice and Tiflis (Tbilisi). Awakening of national consciousness. Khachatur Abovyan is the father of modern Armenian literature, the author of the first novel in conversational Armenian. "Wounds of Armenia" (1841) — about the suffering of Armenians under Persian and Turkish rule.

XIX - beginning of XX century. bloom and tragedy:

Raffi (Rafael Patkanyan) - historical novels, patriotism. Hovhannes Tumanyan - the most favorite folk poet, storyteller. Siamanto , Danzig Mikaelyan,Daniel Varuzhan - representatives of "blood poetry" - tragic poets killed during the Genocide of 1915.

Soviet Armenian literature (1920–1991)

Rise and repression. development of literary schools, but also strict censorship. Egishe Charents - modernist, repressed in 1937. Paruyr Sevak - philosophical and civil lyrics. Silva Kaputikyan - poetess, activist, important figure of the 60s and 70s. Vaan Teryan, Av. Isahakyan - romantics and lyrics.

Contemporary and diasporic literature (XX-XXI centuries)

Diaspora played a huge role after the genocide. William Saroyan - American writer of Armenian origin, winner of the Pulitzer Prize. Nanor Balayan, Kevork Emin , Vakhan Tekeyyan - literature of exile and memory. Contemporary authors in Armenia. Armen Hovhannisyan, Armen Shekoyan, Granush Kharatyan and others.
